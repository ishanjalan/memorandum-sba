Memorandum on Women's Safety in Police Procedures

A call for procedural reforms to protect women complainants in Karnataka police stations and courts

Our Nine Demands

  1. 1. Protection of personal information in police complaints

    Women who file complaints at police stations must have their personal information — including home address, workplace, and contact details — kept strictly confidential. This information must not be shared with the accused or any third party without the complainant's explicit consent.

  2. 2. Strict disciplinary action for leakage of complainants' personal information

    Any police officer found to have disclosed the personal information of a woman complainant to the accused or their associates must face prompt and strict departmental and legal action. Clear accountability mechanisms must be established and enforced.

  3. 3. Prohibition on forcing victims to confront accused persons for "settlements"

    Women complainants must not be compelled or pressured to meet the accused in person at police stations or elsewhere for the purpose of reaching informal settlements. Such practices cause further trauma and undermine the complainant's right to justice.

  4. 4. Prioritisation of FIR registration instead of pressured settlements

    Police officers must register First Information Reports (FIRs) promptly when a cognisable offence is disclosed, without steering complainants towards informal or out-of-court settlements. The law must be applied without bias or delay.

  5. 5. Avoiding unnecessary involvement of women's family members or workplaces

    Investigations must be conducted without unnecessarily contacting or involving the complainant's family members, employer, or colleagues, unless directly relevant to the case and with the complainant's knowledge. Such contact often results in secondary victimisation and social harm.

  6. 6. Police assistance in arranging Mahazar witnesses

    When a Mahazar (panchnama) is required, the police must take responsibility for arranging independent and impartial witnesses. Women complainants must not be burdened with producing their own witnesses, which places an unfair and often impossible obligation on them.

  7. 7. Written notice before summoning women to police stations

    Women must be given advance written notice whenever they are required to appear at a police station, stating the reason for their presence, the name and rank of the officer concerned, and the expected date and time. Verbal or informal summons must be discontinued.

  8. 8. Timely information regarding court appearances

    Investigating officers must ensure that women complainants and witnesses receive timely and accurate information about scheduled court hearings in which they are required to appear. Last-minute notices cause unnecessary hardship and can prejudice the complainant's participation in justice proceedings.

  9. 9. Coordination between investigating officers and public prosecutors

    Investigating officers must maintain regular and effective coordination with the assigned public prosecutor throughout the trial. Poor communication between these two arms of the state is a common cause of preventable lapses that weaken prosecution and harm victims.

Specific Request to the Government

We respectfully urge the Home Department, Government of Karnataka to issue an official circular to all police stations, district superintendents of police, and investigative authorities across Karnataka, incorporating the nine safeguards listed above as standard operating procedure. This circular must establish clear accountability, reporting mechanisms, and consequences for non-compliance, so that women who approach the state for protection receive the dignified, safe, and impartial treatment they are entitled to under the law.
